Kaun Banega Codepati

Rules

  • Participants should be enrolled in an educational institution
  • Participants can participate in TEAMS of AT MOST 2 students.
  • Each participant can be a member of only 1 team.
  • There will be TWO rounds.

Registration

  • Fill out the registration form (to be updated)
  • Enter the FULL NAME of team members, College/School, Email IDs, and Degree/Class.

Qualification Round

  • MCQ-based round, four marks for correct answers and -1 for wrong answers.
  • Questions will be based on DSA, time complexity analysis, and debugging.
  • We will share the quiz link with registered teams.
  • Alternatively, teams may opt to attempt the offline quiz.
  • Separate quiz will be held for school and college teams.
  • The duration will be 45 minutes.
  • 15 College teams and 15 School teams will qualify for the final round.

Final Round

  • 2 hrs Coding round, with IOI style leader board (partial scoring with no penalty for failed submissions).
  • The problem set will have 4-6 problems
  • The final rank will be decided by the total time taken to submit the first submission with the maximum score for each problem.
  • Each problem will have time & memory limits for submissions.
  • Teams are allowed to use a 25-page reference code (PDF is permitted). Teams should upload the reference code in the registration form. (Form response is editable, so the teams may fill out the registration form without reference code and upload the code later).
  • Reference code must be submitted at least 1 hour before the Coding round. Teams will be allowed to download the reference code before the Coding round.

KeyNote 1

How do recommendation systems work? And, what are their privacy implications?
Abstract

Have you ever wondered how good Spotify, Netflix, Amazon, .. give you such great recommendations for what to listen, watch, purchase, and live our lives? The magic behind their recommendations are the deep learning machine learning models. These models capture seemingly end-less amounts of information about our online behavior and transform these behaviors into embeddings for future recommendations. These machine learning models are massive (think of terabytes of data), trained on equally imposing set of training samples, called sparse features. Every click, purchase, and even a mouse hover on a website is a sparse feature for training the model. In this talk I will first provide an overview of how current generation recommendation systems work. If these models can recommend so well, then, they must also know a lot about us. In fact, they do. By simply observing features such as click history and object interactions an attacker can de-anonymize users with extremely high probability, or track users across different interaction sessions. For instance, if you tell me what are the last two items you purchased online, I can track you with 97% accuracy. All of which is to say there is a lot of interesting privacy research that needs to get done.
